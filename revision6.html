Origins of Rome

Location, Founding & Greek Influence

How Rome began — from a legendary founding on the Italian peninsula to absorbing Greek culture.

Location

🌍 Where is Rome?

Rome was situated on the western coast of central Italy, on the Italian peninsula in the Mediterranean Sea. Its central location helped it control trade and military routes.

People

👤 Who were the Romans?

The people of Rome were called the Romans. They began as a small community that grew into one of the most powerful civilizations in history.

🐺 The Legend of Romulus & Remus

According to legend, Rome was founded by the twin brothers Romulus and Remus, who were raised by a she-wolf. Romulus later killed Remus and became the first king of Rome. However, this story is considered a legend, not historical fact.

📝 Exam Note: The Romulus & Remus story is a legend, not actual history. You may be asked to identify it as such.
Greek Influence

🏛️ Rome Learned from Greece

Roman civilization was heavily influenced by Greek civilization. The Romans learned from the Greeks in areas such as art, architecture, philosophy, government, and religion, then developed their own distinct Roman civilization.

Political Systems

Political Evolution of Rome

Rome's government changed dramatically over time — from kings to citizens to emperors.

Stage 1

👑 Roman Monarchy

Rome was originally ruled by kings under a monarchy. The king held supreme power, advised by a council of elders.

Stage 2

🏛️ Roman Republic

Romans overthrew a cruel king and established a republic — a state ruled by the people. Male citizens elected their rulers. This system was similar to Athenian democracy.

Stage 3

👑 Roman Empire

After a period of civil wars, Octavian Caesar restored monarchy and became the first emperor. In the empire, emperors held all power; citizens no longer elected leaders. The executive and legislative branches lost their authority.

📝 Key Exam Point: Rome's political system changed from Monarchy → Republic → Monarchy (Empire). Know the reasons for each change.
Government

Structure of the Roman Republic

The Republic divided power so that it would not be held by a single person.

Executive Branch

👤 Consuls & Senate

  • Two consuls were the highest officials, elected annually by male citizens
  • The Senate advised the consuls and controlled finances and foreign policy
  • Consuls commanded the army and presided over the government
Legislative Branch

📜 Legislative Assemblies

  • Legislative assemblies discussed and passed laws
  • All male citizens could participate in these assemblies
  • They voted on laws, elected officials, and declared war or peace

⚖️ Separation of Powers

Power was separated among the consuls, the Senate, and the legislative assemblies. This prevented any single person from holding all the power — a principle that influences modern governments today.

📝 Exam Note: A republic = a state ruled by the people through elected representatives. Rome's republic had two branches: executive (consuls + Senate) and legislative (assemblies).
Transition

Power Struggles & the Rise of the Roman Empire

The Republic collapsed after a series of power struggles between ambitious generals.

Key Figure

⚔️ Julius Caesar

A successful Roman general who defeated the senators and became dictator for life — holding all power in his own hands. He made the Roman Republic more powerful and improved people's lives, gaining support from soldiers and common people. However, he was later assassinated by senators who feared his power.

🤝 Octavian & Antony

After Caesar's death, his adopted son Octavian and general Antony cooperated to defeat the senators and ruled the republic together.

⚔️ Civil War

They eventually fought a civil war. Octavian defeated Antony and became the sole ruler of Rome.

The Empire Begins

👑 Octavian — First Emperor

After his victory, Octavian Caesar restored monarchy and founded the Roman Empire. He became the first emperor. In the empire, emperors held all power; citizens no longer elected leaders.

📝 Key Exam Point: The Republic ended when one person (first Julius Caesar as dictator, then Octavian as emperor) held all the power.
Empire

Golden Age, Division & Decline

At its peak, Rome was the most powerful empire in the ancient world. But internal and external pressures led to its fall.

Golden Age

🌟 The Roman Empire at Its Peak

The Roman Empire represented the golden age of ancient Rome — powerful, wealthy, and expansive. Its vast territory covered Europe, Asia, and Africa. The Mediterranean Sea became the Empire's "inner lake," surrounded entirely by Roman lands.

Division

📅 AD 395 — The Empire is Divided

In AD 395, the Roman Empire was divided into two halves:

  • Western Roman Empire — capital in Rome
  • Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire) — capital in Constantinople
Fall of the West

AD 476

The Western Roman Empire fell to the Germanic peoples from the north. This marked the end of Ancient times and the start of the Medieval period in Europe.

Fall of the East

AD 1453

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine) fell to the Ottoman Turks when Constantinople was conquered. This marked the end of Medieval times and the start of Modern times.

Cultural Heritage

Greek Myths, Olympics, Language & Philosophy

Classical Greek culture laid the foundation for Western civilization in literature, sports, language, and thought.

Myths & Religion

🎭 Greek Mythology

Ancient Greeks worshipped many gods and goddesses. Zeus was the head of the gods. Gods were imagined as half-god, half-man — with human feelings but also great strength and wisdom. These myths provided themes for European literature and drama.

Sport

🏟️ Olympic Games

Greeks believed some gods lived on Mount Olympus. The Olympic Games were held to honour Zeus. During the games, city-states stopped fighting so athletes could compete safely. The Modern Olympic Games continue this tradition every four years.

Language

🗣️ Influence on Languages

Greek myths shaped European languages. Many modern English words come from Greek origins. Example: "Music" comes from the Greek word Muses, goddesses of the arts.

Philosophy

📚 Western Philosophy

The word "philosophy" means "love of wisdom" in Greek. Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle are regarded as the founders of Western philosophy. Their ideas became the foundation of European intellectual traditions.

Art & Architecture

Greek vs Roman Art & Architecture

Both civilizations made lasting contributions to art and building design. Compare their styles.

Architecture

🏛️ Greek Architecture

Important Greek buildings (especially temples) were surrounded by tall stone columns. Pediments (triangular structures at the top) were common. These elements became long-lasting models adopted by architects for centuries.

Architecture

🏟️ Roman Architecture

Roman architecture was characterized by domes and arches. Roman cities were well-planned with extensive road networks linking Rome to its provinces. They built diverse facilities: temples, forums, theatres, stadiums, and bathhouses.

Legacy

Governance & Legal Systems

The political and legal ideas of Greece and Rome continue to shape modern democracies and legal systems.

Greek Legacy

🏛️ Athenian Democracy

Athenian democracy was the most open political system of its time. Key features: public participation and secret ballot. Many modern countries draw inspiration from this model.

Roman Legacy

🏟️ Republican System

A republic = a state ruled by the people. The Roman republican system spread from Rome to Europe and still influences modern countries today.

Centralization

🏛️ Administrative Centralization

The Roman imperial system emphasized centralized administration. In modern states, the central government holds supreme power while local governments derive authority from it.

Rule of Law

⚖️ Law & Justice

Athenians valued civil rights and law. Romans later adopted the idea of rule of law from Greece. Romans believed all citizens were equal before the law with rights to a fair trial and to appeal. Many modern countries adopted the jury system from these traditions.

📝 Exam Strategy: Questions often ask how Greek and Roman political ideas influenced modern governments. Focus on: democracy (Greece), republic (Rome), separation of powers (Rome), rule of law, and the jury system.
